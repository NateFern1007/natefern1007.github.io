Introduction:

Being a manager is by no means an easy job, and can be quite stressful. Some managers might be responsible for multiple teams, having to constantly juggle numerous projects at once, while others might be responsible for managing an entire division. Either way, as a manager it is in your best interests to provide the resources and tools your employees need to allow them to be successful in the workplace. It is hard to acknowledge, but many managers and businesses today still find it challenging to motivate their employees. However, motivation is a leading factor in reducing employee burnout within an organization, so understanding how to improve motivation is a key business skill.

Overview of Evidence:

Evidence suggests that people are more likely to be motivated when they believe that: 1) [an employee’s] behavior will lead to certain rewards, 2) the rewards are worthwhile and valuable, and 3) employees can perform at a level that will result in the attainment of rewards  [1] [2] Furthermore, motivation is also driven by “the relation of beliefs, values, and goals.”[3] 

Since motivation is a behavioral trait, managers must understand how to effectively target and redirect undesired behavior towards the behavior that the organization desires. In doing so, managers can further help their employees expand and build upon their existing skills, reduce employee burnout, and increase their overall performance and job satisfaction.

The Framework (Social Cognitive Theory)

Our journey begins with social cognitive theory (SCT), a behavioral framework that shows triadic reciprocal causation between behavior, people, and environment[4] [5] 

From: (Bandura and Wood)[6]

Despite the relationships between behavior, people, and environment, not every pillar in SCT is always going to be present, nor is each pillar weighted with the same level of magnitude. It is therefore important for managers to realize that there are additional factors that can prompt or change employees’ behavior based on the external environment around them. The three pillars shown in the figure above are not the only factors that exist within the greater framework of social cognitive theory. In addition, there are five basic human capabilities (note figure below), which can further explain triadic reciprocal causation of SCT. By understanding these basic capabilities, managers can better target and redirect an employee’s behavior before it gets out of hand.

From: (Stadjkovic 1998)[7]

Looking at SCT before looking will help managers understand not only where burnout originates, but how they can strategically mitigate burnout from occurring in their organization.

Employee Burnout

Based on empirical evidence, burnout can be associated with one’s behavior and the external environment[8] and is composed of three components: exhaustion, cynicism, and inefficacy.[9] [10] Exhaustion affects one’s ability to effectively carry out their goals, leading to a reduction in performance. Employee performance is heavily impacted by burnout because of inefficacy, or a reduction in one’s self-efficacy. Therefore, it is critical for managers to understand how to target and increase an employee’s self-efficacy to help them break away from the vicious cycle of burnout.

Self-Efficacy Theory:

Self-efficacy is the last basic human capability (self-regulation) within the greater framework of social cognitive theory. It, along with other behavioral theories, is controlled by several regulatory mechanisms that operate together.[11] While there are multiple definitions of self-efficacy, an example is “an individual’s confidence in their ability to organize and execute a given course of action to solve a problem or accomplish a task.”[12] [13] An employee who has low self-efficacy because of employee burnout, would likely find their ability to solve a problem and/or accomplish a task to be quite challenging. This is not because the employee is incompetent or does not possess the aptitude that is required to get to the end result; rather it has to do with the difference in expectations between what the employee imagined and what realistically happened. Managers must therefore address how the changes in employee’s expectations can affect their ability to increase self-efficacy and re-stimulate motivation.

Expectancy Theories (Attribution and Self-Worth):

Expectancies can also influence one’s motivation because of the expectations that are set with having to complete a task. Psychologists and management scholars coined the term expectancy theory, which is made up of two components that work together to help further understand how expectations can affect the motivation process.  Attribution theory and self-worth theory can affect one's expectation outcomes, which can lead to changes in one’s overall behavior. Eccles and Wigfield, defined attribution theory as “an individual’s interpretation of their achievement outcomes, rather than motivational dispositions or actual outcomes, determine subsequent achievement strivings.”[14] This difference in interpretation is what decreases one’s self-efficacy because “even before the amount of effort is considered, employees tend to weigh, evaluate, and integrate information about their perceived capabilities.”[15]  This sudden change in perceived capabilities can further affect an employee’s self-efficacy because now there is a difference in outcomes that were not evaluated during the forethought process. This affects one’s overall self-worth because of the “tendency to establish and maintain a positive self-image or sense of … to protect one’s sense of academic competence”.[16] Therefore, when employees see themselves as having lower perceived capabilities, their cynicism increases because they thought that they could do better. Cynicism impacts the employee’s self-efficacy because of the undesired lower perceived expectancy, which further leads to a decrease in their perceived capabilities. It is essentially a vicious cycle.

As managers, we must therefore ask ourselves “how do we break this cycle of burnout?” and “is it even possible?” The short answer is, “yes, it is” but to have this work, the manager also must take a role.

Goal Setting

One of the most effective ways that a manager can support and motivate their employees out of this burnout cycle is by setting goals. Most managers would resort to using performance goals, which is fine, but at this stage, is not the optimal response. This is because the employee is not at a stage where they feel that their ability to complete a performance goal aligns with the expectations that would be set. This might be because a prior performance goal was not attained in a satisfactory manner, which affects their efficacy expectations. (Refer back to Expectancy Outcomes).

Evidence suggests that using performance goals is not the best source of goal setting techniques because they are a way of demonstrating superiority rather than competency.[17] [18] In addition, because individuals tend to rehearse out task strategies until they become automatic[19] as a result of the forethought capability, when efficacy expectations change, it can have an overall effect on the development of a person’s self-efficacy. This is why managers need to implement mastery/learning goal setting techniques.  These goal setting techniques focus on individual attention and the development of new knowledge and processing strategies.[20] [21] They can further assist in reprogramming an employee’s struggling self-regulatory and self-reflective capabilities to further boost confidence and competency by setting goals that push for improvement.

Mastery Orientated Goals:

Mastery goal setting or learning goals, are a excellent goal setting techniques because they allow the employee to build on growing their competency. Managers can help employees who are suffering from burnout and low motivation, set new goals that are tailored towards what skills they would like to improve on or more importantly, what interests them the most. This form of goal setting avoids skill loss, leaving a task incomplete, or unmastered.[22] It allows employees to continue their development and competency while working, which can lead to them being intrinsically motivated because they will want to extend their skills based on what they think they need to work on. Prior research has already shown that those who tend to be more intrinsically motivated will want to further engage in more challenging problems and are more willing to take calculated risks as they find the work that they do to be much more meaningful. (Latham 2018)[23] however, newer research presented in the last few months has presented a third form of motivation (see Locke and Schattke).

Mindset and Competence Theories:

Being engaged and motivated is a perpetuating loop because it can further influence an employee’s mindset (behavior) cause them to be more willing to take the extra effort to stretch themselves further (Dweck et. al. 2016, 5).[24] This stretching of the mindset allows for employees to take more calculated risks and can increase their self-efficacy because they believe that they are going to grow their competency and achieve their learning goals and/or objectives. When an employee stretches beyond their boundaries to take a calculated risk, not only are they growing their competency, but they are further expanding and developing their self-efficacy skills, feeling more empowered and intrinsically motivated, and have higher performance. Therefore, competency is important to one’s understanding because it can further affect one’s self-esteem Dweck 2017[25] 

Self-esteem and self-efficacy exist within the expectancy attribution models, which are critical in fueling motivation. People who have fixed mindsets will find it very challenging to improve and take on mastery goals because they are stuck with the understanding that there is no room for improvement. This can lead to increased burnout, reducing self-efficacy which is not what manager wants to see in their employees. On the flipside, employees with growth mindsets realize that sometimes you make mistakes and that frankly, a failure is more or less the first attempt at learning. This incentivizes employees to take that failure and turn it into something worthwhile.

Forms of Motivation:

Intrinsic and Extrinsic Motivation:

It is in a manager’s best interest to try to drive the tasks, objectives, and goals towards the kind of motivation that the employee looks for. Research conducted on employees being driven by intrinsic interests  show greater success in being able to set higher attainable goals, because they tend to be more effective. (Janssen 370)[26] An employee driven by intrinsic motivation wants to grow and further develop competency in a skill, rather than working for a reward that is external to the task at hand.

Extrinsic motivation is quite opposite to intrinsic motivation and instead focuses on “engage[ing] in activities for instrumental reasons such as receiving a reward.”[27] This usually results in slower performance because the employee is not really engaged in doing the task, rather they are just going through the motions while waiting for an external reward. In many cases, this distracts the employee from being able to efficiently complete the task at hand, which leads to slower performance.[28] [29] The reduced performance can have an effect on their behavior and/or sense of competence because the amount of effort that they have to put into completing the task is greater than the perceived worth, which reduces their self-worth and self-efficacy. To avoid this, managers should be aware that some people are motivated differently and be able to adapt.

Where is Current Research Now?

Latham and Locke proposed a model of working motivation theory in 2004 (see appendix) to help explain the different connections that have been made within the field of management. They specifically mention that the connection between needs and values needs more empirical evidence. Dweck’s model of goals and needs provides a possible linkage between needs and values based on the figure below.

From: (Dweck 2017)[30]

Dweck argues that these are the basic needs that people must have met, and includes three additional needs (Predictability, Competence, and Acceptance). Her theory builds on top of Maslow (Psychological, Safety, Belonging, Self-Esteem, Self-Actualization) and McClelland’s need’s theory (Achievement, Affiliation, Power) (Dweck 2017) to help further describe why needs are important in understanding ones motivation and overall behavior. In addition, Dweck also proposed a new foundation for connecting one’s motivation to their personality through beliefs, emotions, actions, and tendencies (BEATS) (see diagram below) Her intention is to provide more clarity between how ones values and personality can be connected back to basic needs.  

From: (Dweck 2017)[31]

Referring to the model that Locke and Latham proposed (see appendix), they mentioned that further would be needed to make a connection between one’s needs and their values as being a component of maintaining one’s motivation. Similarly, they also note similar definition of motivation to Pardee 1990, in which they explain that “The concept of motivation refers to internal factors that impel action and to external factors that can act as inducements to action. The three aspects of action that motivation can affect are direction (choice), intensity (effort), and duration (persistence).” (Locke and Latham 2004 1). Since motivation is driven by an action (either as external or internal) it is important to recognize the connection between how goal setting has been used as a motivational technique.

While this model is older than many of the other resources that were used in this paper, it does provide a good outline of the components that are needed in order to maintain motivation in the workplace. Other findings from Locke and Schattke pose a possible relationship between intrinsic, extrinsic, and achievement motivation.

Locke and Schattke argue a slightly different view on intrinsic motivation, where internal stimulation or action is built off of interest or pleasure. (Locke and Schattke 2019, 280)[32] Furthermore, they note that the goal is pleasure or enjoyment rather than some outcome or achievement.[33] They also claim that researchers are getting confused by combining what they consider to be achievement motivation with intrinsic interests, and different states in which motivation could occur. Subconscious priming through goal setting orientations and social cognitive theory are some examples discussed.[34] Considering that this is a relatively new study in the field, both Locke and Schattke make an interesting argument for how research might progress further in the future that could potentially explain a third possible pillar of motivation that creates a triadic relationship similar to SCT (see the model below).

Concluding Remarks:

If a manager wants to re-motivate their employees, they must know how to reprogram the basic capabilities of self-realization and self-reflection through mastery goal setting. This can further provide the employee with developing their competency and increasing self-efficacy. (Dweck 2016; Dweck 2017; Janssen 2014; Bandura 1988; Stadjkovic 1998; Eccles and Wigfield 2002) Given that mastery goals are more focused on self-development rather than performance, this reduces attribution and expectancy errors because the employee is setting goals based on their needs and wants. Pulling from Dweck (2017), managers need to recognize their employee’s needs, and how they can be satisfied, in addition to their personality and mindset so the employee sets attainable goals for themselves. From there, a manager can leverage points from motivation theory (Locke & Latham 2004) to further restart the engagement process and empower the employee to push forward. Last but not least, the five basic capabilities and the triadic relationship of SCT can provide insight for managers because it can help them identify where in the process their employees might be struggling, and how to redirect their behavior.

As mentioned above, Dweck has already investigated this within her own research but developing a model that explains the fundamental needs of workers and makes a  clear argument between the connection of values and personality. (Dweck 2017), Furthermore, SCT is gaining more understanding among scholars and is being used more widely within the realms of organizational behavior and management frameworks. Thus, managers and researchers need to devise a mechanism or model that can effectively target employee’s motivation before burnout occurs as a risk preventative measure. Lastly, a new addition to motivation might be arising as mentioned by (Locke and Schtattke 2019). While their article is still fairly recent, it has gotten some attention leading to possible breakthrough in motivation theory which has not really changed in the last twenty years.

Recommendation:

An understanding of the process of motivation is critical for any manager in any organization. Motivation is not just the internal behavior of an employee, but stems also from SCT, self-efficacy, mindset, goals, psychological, and cognitive processes. Since a manager’s job is to promote, maintain, and utilize each employee’s unique potential, (Professor Claire)[36] it is important for them to understand how each person is motivated, and how to provide the tools for them to want to be motivated. In doing so, they can build a workplace that promotes high performance, low burnout, and high job satisfaction. The steps mentioned above can work together to break the cycle of employee burnout. In doing so, employees will feel more satisfied with their job, take larger calculated risks, gain more mastery in a particular skill, continue to develop behaviors that promote growth and learning, and last but not least, feel motivated to not only do their job but to push others to do the same.
